Who he is — in one paragraph

Prof. Sanjeev Prashar is a Marketing professor at IIM Raipur since 2012, with a PhD from Kurukshetra University. His self-declared expertise is Buying Behaviour, Impulse Buying, Online Buying, Mall Selection, Rural Marketing, International Marketing, and Marketing of Services. He has 76+ journal articles and an h-index of 33 on Google Scholar. He almost always co-authors with PhD scholars (Chandan Parsad, T. Sai Vijay, Sudhir Rana, Vishal Shukla, S.V. Tata, Nilesh Arora appear repeatedly), which suggests he is a mentorship-heavy researcher.

His actual research domain (in simple words)

He studies why people buy what they buy — especially in Indian retail and online contexts. His questions are practical: What makes someone walk into a mall and buy something unplanned? Why does one e-commerce website convert better than another? What makes a customer come back? What makes a celebrity endorsement work or fail? Does a "Like" on social media actually translate into a purchase?

Most of his work uses structured surveys with consumers, then runs statistical models to test which factors really drive the outcome.

Major recurring themes

  • Online shopping behaviour — hedonic vs utilitarian value, website cues (S–O–R framework)
  • Impulse buying — store atmospherics, mood, personality traits
  • Mall & retail experience — shopping experience anatomy, mall clustering
  • Post-purchase behaviour — satisfaction, regret, repurchase intention, e-loyalty
  • Celebrity endorsement — congruency, attitude, purchase intention
  • eWOM / social media — reviews, social capital, purchase intention
  • International marketing (EMNCs) — GAMBIT scale
  • Recent tech turn — virtual influencers, AR/VR tourism, blockchain & tokenisation, sentiment analysis on online reviews

Research philosophy — inferred from his papers

  • Empirical & structured. He prefers measurable constructs and tests hypotheses with statistics. He is not a "pure theory" researcher.
  • Indian-context first. Most samples are Indian shoppers, Indian malls, Indian e-retailers. He treats India as a research site worth understanding on its own terms.
  • Practical implications matter. Most papers end with a section addressed to retailers, marketers, and managers. He wants research that someone can use.
  • Mentorship-heavy. Pattern: one PhD scholar + Prof. Prashar + one senior co-author.
  • Methodological openness — recently. Older papers use SEM/CFA/regression. Newer papers bring in ANN, sentiment analysis, web scraping, bibliometric tools, TISM, fsQCA, even blockchain proofs-of-concept. He is clearly willing to absorb tech-enabled methods.

What I genuinely understood about Prof. Prashar's work

This page is the "honest reading" — what I would actually say if he asks "So, what did you understand from my papers?"

1. He is fundamentally a consumer behaviour researcher, not a "tech" researcher

Even his recent papers on blockchain, ANN or virtual influencers are still about the same core question — what drives consumer choice and post-purchase behaviour. The technology is the stimulus or the context; the dependent variable is almost always something like satisfaction, intention to purchase, intention to repurchase, loyalty, or regret.

2. He uses a small, repeatable research recipe

Across many of his papers I noticed the same pattern:

  1. Pick a real consumer setting (mall, online retailer, social media, refurbished phones).
  2. Identify stimuli (e.g., store atmospherics, web cues, celebrity attributes, social influence).
  3. Identify an organism variable in the middle (satisfaction, attitude, emotional state).
  4. Identify a response outcome (purchase intention, impulse buying, repurchase, regret).
  5. Test the chain with SEM (often AMOS, sometimes PLS-SEM).
  6. End with implications for retailers and managers.

The 2017 Vikalpa paper using the S–O–R framework is almost a template for this. Once you see the recipe, you can read his other papers much faster.

3. His Indian context is not incidental — it is central

He repeatedly samples from Indian metros (Delhi, Mumbai, Kolkata, Bengaluru, Hyderabad) and from Indian business school students. He treats India as a market with its own behavioural patterns, not as a test bed for Western theories. The 2019 e-loyalty paper, for example, finds that hedonic shopping value did not drive e-satisfaction in India, which is the opposite of many Western findings. That kind of nuance is what he seems to care about.

4. The "Beyond Likes!" paper shows where his work is heading

The 2025 AAMJ paper with Nilesh Arora and Sudhir Rana is interesting because it does not assume "more likes / more friends = more sales". It actually finds that normative influence and bonding social capital are not significant predictors of eWOM, while informational influence, trust and bridging social capital are. That is a non-obvious finding and shows he is willing to publish results that contradict marketing folk-wisdom.

5. The GAMBIT paper shows the other side of his research

GAMBIT is much more managerial and strategy-oriented. It is essentially scale development for international marketing strategy in Indian manufacturing EMNCs. Different audience, different method (Churchill's 8-step scale development), but the same underlying instinct — turn a fuzzy managerial question into a measurable, validated instrument.

6. What I personally found interesting

  • The 2024 refurbished smartphone paper with web scraping, sentiment analysis and stepwise regression — because it is the closest to my own technical skill set, and because the finding ("buyers care more about usage duration than total life; negative text reviews dissuade more than star ratings") is a genuinely useful, slightly counter-intuitive result.
  • The post-purchase regret stream — because it studies what happens after the sale, which most marketing research skips.
  • The fact that he is comfortable mixing classical SEM with ANN in the same paper (e.g., ecotourism / PER) — it tells me he is open to a researcher saying "let me run a small ANN as a robustness check" without thinking I am over-engineering.
The honest line I can use on the call:
"Sir, after reading a few of your papers I see a very consistent style — you take a real consumer situation, pin down the stimuli and the psychological mediator, and test the chain empirically with Indian data. I am not coming in as a marketing researcher; I am coming in as someone who can help with the parts of that pipeline that are data and tooling heavy — scraping, cleaning, visualising, and possibly light NLP where it genuinely fits."

📄 Effects of Online Shopping Values and Website Cues on Purchase Behaviour — A Study Using S–O–R Framework 2017 · Vikalpa Full paper
Authors
Sanjeev Prashar, T. Sai Vijay, Chandan Parsad
Year & Journal
2017 · Vikalpa: The Journal for Decision Makers, 42(1), 1–18
Research problem
What drives online purchase behaviour of Indian shoppers — is it the website cues, the shopping values, or the satisfaction they generate?
Objective
Test a S–O–R chain: shopping values + website cues → website satisfaction → purchase intention, in the Indian e-retail context.
Theoretical lens
Stimulus–Organism–Response (Mehrabian & Russell)
Constructs
Stimulus: Hedonic shopping value, Utilitarian shopping value, Web informativeness, Web entertainment, Effectiveness of information content
Organism: Website satisfaction
Response: Purchase intention
Sample
318 usable responses (from 404 collected) · 5 Indian metros — Delhi, Mumbai, Kolkata, Bengaluru, Hyderabad · respondents had ≥5 prior online transactions
Methodology
Survey + intercept; SEM in AMOS 22.0; two-step CFA then structural model
Key findings
• All five stimuli significantly increased website satisfaction.
• Website satisfaction strongly drives purchase intention.
• Website satisfaction fully mediates the relationship from stimuli to purchase intention.
• Gender and income did not significantly affect satisfaction.
Limitations
Convenience sampling; cross-sectional design; only metro Indian shoppers.
Practical implication
For e-retailers: website satisfaction is the bridge from any marketing stimulus to actual purchase. Invest in information quality and ease of use before entertainment.
In simple words
If your website helps people get things done and feels good to use, they end up satisfied — and satisfied users are the ones who actually buy.
My honest read
This paper is the template for many of his other studies. Once I understood the S–O–R recipe here, the 2019 e-loyalty paper looked like a sibling.
How I might contribute to similar work
Scrape live web cues (load time, info density, entertainment-style modules) across a sample of e-retailers; cluster sites; help a PhD scholar design a follow-up study with objective rather than self-reported stimulus measures.
📄 The Influence of Online Shopping Values and Web Atmospheric Cues on E-Loyalty — Mediating Role of E-Satisfaction 2019 · JTAER Full paper
Authors
T. Sai Vijay, Sanjeev Prashar, V. Sahay
Year & Journal
2019 · Journal of Theoretical and Applied Electronic Commerce Research, 14(1), 1–15
Research problem
Does e-satisfaction mediate the link between online shopping values / web atmospheric cues and e-loyalty, in the Indian online retail context?
Constructs
Independent: Hedonic value, Utilitarian value, Informativeness, Effectiveness of information content, Entertainment
Mediator: E-satisfaction
Dependent: E-loyalty
Sample
200 usable responses from online shoppers, students at a premier business school in central India
Methodology
SEM in AMOS 22.0; CFA + structural model
Key findings
• Utilitarian shopping value significantly increases e-satisfaction.
• Hedonic shopping value did not significantly affect e-satisfaction (interesting Indian-context finding).
• All three web cues — informativeness, effectiveness, entertainment — significantly drove e-satisfaction.
• E-satisfaction strongly drives e-loyalty and mediates the upstream relationships.
• Effectiveness of information content had the strongest total effect on e-loyalty.
Limitations
Student sample; one institution; cross-sectional; small N (200).
Practical implication
For Indian e-retailers: focus on information clarity and utility — that is the lever for loyalty in markets where switching costs are low.
In simple words
In India, online shoppers come back not because shopping was "fun" but because the site was useful and clear. Fun matters less than function.
My honest read
The non-significance of hedonic value is the most interesting line in the paper for me — it pushes back on a finding that is taken for granted in Western literature.
How I might contribute to similar work
Help replicate this with a non-student, broader Indian sample, possibly through online panels; build the survey data pipeline so multiple PhD scholars can iterate on item wording.
📄 Determinants of International Marketing Strategy for Emerging-Market Multinationals (GAMBIT model) 2021 · IJoEM Full paper
Authors
Sudhir Rana, Sanjeev Prashar, Munim Kumar Barai, Abu Bakar Abdul Hamid
Year & Journal
2021 · International Journal of Emerging Markets, 16(2), 154–178
What GAMBIT stands for
Generating Assessment Model for Benefit Impacts generated by International Marketing (taken loosely — note: spelling expansion varies in the paper)
Research problem
EMNCs (Tata, Infosys, Mahindra etc.) internationalise with latecomer disadvantages and have no standardised, validated instrument to measure how their international marketing strategy translates into outcomes.
Four determinants in the model
1. International Marketing Sources — foundational capabilities and resources
2. International Marketing Operational Efficiency — execution quality, perfect order fulfilment
3. International Marketing Strategic Choice — market selection, entry mode, 4Ps
4. International Marketing Beneficial Outcomes — measurable results
Methodology
Quantitative scale development using Churchill's 8-step paradigm. EFA → CFA → SEM in SPSS / AMOS. Sample: international marketing & senior executives in Indian manufacturing firms.
Key findings
Hierarchical chain: Sources → Operational Efficiency → Strategic Choice → Beneficial Outcomes. "Perfect order fulfilment" and "quality level" emerge as central bridging variables.
Theoretical contribution
A validated, EMNC-specific scale that consolidates fragmented prior frameworks.
Managerial contribution
A diagnostic checklist for boards / leadership of EMNCs going global; a benchmarking instrument across regions.
Limitations
Indian manufacturing only · cross-sectional · "low-tech fit": doesn't address platform / digital-first internationalisation.
In simple words
If an Indian company wants to figure out whether its global marketing is actually working, this paper gives them a structured 4-step diagnostic.
My honest read
This is the most strategy-flavoured paper in the set, and it shows that Prof. Prashar is not only a consumer-behaviour researcher — he also works on managerial / strategy scale-development.
How I might contribute to similar work
Build a small web-based tool that lets EMNC managers self-assess against the four determinants and produces a benchmark report — this is closer to "research tool building" than "primary research", and it is honest.
📄 Beyond Likes! Investigating the Influence of Social Relationships on eWOM and Purchase Intention in Social Media 2025 · AAMJ Full paper
Authors
Nilesh Arora, Sudhir Rana, Meghna Rana, Sanjeev Prashar, Huam Hon Tat
Year & Journal
2025 · Asian Academy of Management Journal, 30(2), 1–37
Research problem
Prior work on eWOM and purchase intention has overlooked how social relationships actually form on SNSs. Likes ≠ influence. Which social-relationship antecedents truly drive eWOM and downstream buying?
Theoretical lens
Uses and Gratification Theory (UGT)
Constructs
Independent: Informative influence, Normative influence, Bridging social capital, Bonding social capital, Perceived homophily, Trust
Mediator-like outcome: eWOM intention
Dependent: Purchase intention
Moderators tested: Social support, Gender
Methodology
Survey of 383 respondents across 5 Indian cities; convenience sampling; PLS-SEM in SmartPLS 3.2.4
Hypotheses results (highlights)
✅ Informative influence → eWOM   (β = 0.269, p = 0.000)
❌ Normative influence → eWOM   (β = −0.076, n.s.)
✅ Bridging social capital → eWOM   (β = 0.280, p = 0.000)
❌ Bonding social capital → eWOM   (β = 0.131, n.s.)
❌ Homophily → eWOM   (n.s.)
✅ Trust → eWOM   (β = 0.259, p = 0.000)
✅ eWOM → Purchase intention   (β = 0.432, p = 0.000)
❌ Social support / gender moderation   (n.s.)
Key takeaway
On social media, information-quality and trust beat peer pressure and "likes" as drivers of eWOM and purchase intention.
Limitations
Indian Millennials & Gen Z only; cultural/contextual limits; no psychological-trait moderators.
In simple words
People share product opinions and end up buying not because they feel social pressure to like a brand, but because they trust the messenger and find the content informative. Likes alone are noise.
My honest read
This is methodologically the most "modern" paper of the four — PLS-SEM, clear hypothesis tests, a non-trivial finding. It also shows Prof. Prashar is comfortable in the social-media / influencer research stream.
How I might contribute to similar work
Augment survey-only evidence with actual social-media text — pull real eWOM posts, run sentiment + theme tagging, and let the PhD scholar cross-check against self-reported survey items. This is exactly the kind of mixed-method extension that is naturally engineering-heavy.

Methodologies explained simply

Goal: be able to nod intelligently when he mentions any of these — not to pretend you can run them.

SEM — Structural Equation Modeling

What it is: A statistical method that tests a whole network of relationships at once — e.g., "A → B → C" — including hidden constructs measured by multiple survey items.

Inputs: Survey responses (Likert-scale items) from typically 200+ respondents.

Outputs: Path coefficients (how strongly A drives B), significance, model-fit indices.

Tools: AMOS, LISREL (covariance-based), SmartPLS (PLS-based).

How technical: Conceptually accessible, technically dense. He uses it heavily.

CFA / EFA — Factor Analysis

EFA looks at a bunch of survey items and discovers which ones group together (you don't tell it the groups). CFA tests whether items load on the groups you expected.

Why he uses them: To validate that, say, "5 items about utilitarian value" really do measure one thing.

Output: Factor loadings, reliability (Cronbach's α), variance explained.

Mall-intercept survey

What it is: Researchers literally stand in malls and ask shoppers (often carrying a shopping bag) to fill a questionnaire.

Why he uses it: Gives access to real shoppers in the actual buying context, not just college students.

Limitations: Convenience sample; mall-skewed demographics; difficult to standardise.

Cluster analysis

What it is: Group respondents (or malls, or products) into segments based on similarity, without pre-defining the segments.

Why he uses it: e.g., the mall-experience papers segment shoppers by what they value.

Tools: SPSS, R, scikit-learn (k-means, hierarchical).

Stepwise / Hierarchical regression

What it is: Add predictors one by one (or in blocks) and see which ones actually improve the explanation of the outcome.

Why he uses it: Quick way to test a relatively small model — e.g., celebrity endorsement paper, smartphone-exchange paper.

ANN — Artificial Neural Networks (in his recent papers)

What it is: A non-linear predictive model. In his work, ANN is used as a complement to SEM, not a replacement — usually to show that non-linear interactions also explain the outcome.

Typical setup: Small MLP, 1–2 hidden layers, mean-squared error or classification accuracy reported.

Honest note for me: This is the one ML technique where my background directly applies and is welcome in his methodology.

Bibliometric analysis

What it is: Quantitative mapping of a research field — citation patterns, co-authorship networks, keyword clusters.

Tools: VOSviewer, Bibliometric R / Biblioshiny.

Inputs: BibTeX / RIS exports from Scopus or Web of Science.

Why he uses it: SLR papers — creative industries, virtual influencers, TAM in retail.

Sentiment analysis on consumer text

What it is: Classifying text reviews as positive / negative / neutral, or scoring emotional polarity.

How he uses it: 2024 smartphone-exchange paper. Negative text reviews mattered more than star ratings.

How I can contribute: VADER / transformer models / LLM-based labelling, always with a human-validated subset.

TISM & Fuzzy-MICMAC

What it is: Total Interpretive Structural Modeling + dependency-driving power analysis — used in operations-management style papers to figure out which factors drive others.

Honest note: I don't need to do these — only recognise them if he mentions them.

fsQCA — Fuzzy-set Qualitative Comparative Analysis

What it is: Finds combinations of conditions (not single variables) that produce an outcome. Used in his blockchain paper.

Honest note: Recognition-level only.
